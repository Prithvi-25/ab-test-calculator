📚 Learn the Basics

How A/B Testing Works

A beginner-friendly guide to understanding the math behind your experiments.

🔬
What is A/B Testing?

A/B testing is a method of comparing two versions of something to figure out which one performs better. You split your users randomly into two groups — one sees Version A (the current design), the other sees Version B (your change) — and measure which group achieves a better outcome (like more sign-ups or clicks).

The key question this calculator answers: How many users do you need before you can trust the results?

📐
Why Sample Size Matters

If you flip a coin 5 times and get 4 heads, you wouldn't conclude the coin is unfair. But if you flip it 1,000 times and get 800 heads — that's meaningful.

A/B testing works the same way. Too few visitors and your results could just be noise. Too many and you're wasting time. This calculator finds the sweet spot — the minimum sample size needed to make a confident decision.

⚙️
The 5 Key Parameters
1. Baseline Conversion Rate

How well your page converts right now. If 5 out of every 100 visitors sign up, your baseline is 5%. You can find this in your analytics dashboard.

2. Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE)

The smallest improvement you care about. A 10% MDE on a 5% baseline means you're trying to detect a lift from 5.0% to 5.5%. Smaller MDE = longer test but catches subtle wins.

3. Daily Traffic

How many visitors each variation gets per day. This determines how long your test needs to run. More traffic = faster results.

4. Statistical Significance (Confidence)

How sure you want to be that the winner is actually better, not just lucky. 95% is standard — meaning only a 5% chance the result is random noise (a "false positive").

5. Statistical Power

The probability of detecting a real improvement when one exists. 80% is standard — meaning if Version B is truly better, you'll correctly detect it 80% of the time.

⚠️
Common Mistakes
✗

Peeking early — Checking results before reaching your sample size inflates false positives. Decide upfront and commit.

✗

Stopping early for a "winner" — Random fluctuations can make one variant look better temporarily. Let the test run its full duration.

✗

Testing too many things at once — Change one variable at a time. Otherwise you won't know what caused the difference.

💡
Pro Tips
✓

Start with big MDE — Test for 20-30% improvements first. You'll get results in days, not weeks. Refine later.

✓

Run for full weeks — User behavior changes by day-of-week. Round your test duration up to the nearest full week.

✓

Document everything — Record your hypothesis, metrics, start/end dates, and results. Builds team learning over time.
